# SSDI & Medicare Continuation Rules

**Key question:** If a client loses their disability cash benefits (SSDI), do they lose Medicare?

**Short answer:** Not always right away. It depends entirely on *why* SSDI was stopped.

---

## Why and When Medicare Stops

### 1. Stopped because of work (Substantial Gainful Activity)
If cash benefits stop because your client went back to work and earns too much, they can usually **keep their free Medicare Part A and Part B for at least 93 months (7 years and 9 months)** from the start of their Trial Work Period — as long as their disabling condition still meets eligibility rules.

**After the 93 months:** They can remain on Medicare but will have to **pay for Part A** — until they turn 65 and become Medicare-eligible based on age.

**2026 SGA income limits:**
- Most beneficiaries: **$1,690/month**
- Blind beneficiaries: **$2,830/month**

### 2. Stopped because of medical improvement
If Social Security reviews the case and decides the client's medical condition has improved enough that they are no longer disabled, **Medicare generally ends shortly after the disability cash benefits stop.**

The SSA does periodic reviews (called Continuing Disability Reviews) to determine ongoing medical eligibility.

### 3. Age exception
If your client turns **65 while on disability**, their Medicare switches to regular age-based Medicare. This coverage **cannot be taken away** just because their disability status changes.

---

## Trial Work Period — Important Protection

Clients do NOT lose SSDI or Medicare during a **trial work period** — a Social Security incentive that lets them earn more than the SGA limit for any **9 months over a 5-year period.**

- During the trial period: keeps SSDI + Medicare fully
- After trial period ends, if still working at/above SGA: loses SSDI but keeps **free Part A for 93 months** (as long as they still have a qualifying disability)

---

## Prior Period of Disability Credit

If a client had a previous period of disability, that time can be **credited toward their Medicare start date** for a new disability onset — if:
- The new onset occurs **less than 5 years** after the previous SSDI ended, OR
- It is due to the **same or closely related medical condition**

---

## Next Steps When a Client's SSDI Is Stopped

1. **Check the official SSA notice letter** — it will state the exact reason benefits were stopped
2. **Look for a specific end date** for medical/hospital coverage
3. **Ask two key questions:**
   - Why was SSDI stopped — returned to work, or medical improvement?
   - How old are they? (65+ = protected under age-based Medicare)
